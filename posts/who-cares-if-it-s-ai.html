Who Cares If It's AI? Judge the Truth, Not the Typewriter

Look, I get it. You're scrolling through Medium, or Substack, or whatever digital content graveyard you prefer, and you see that telltale shine. That uncanny smoothness. That suspiciously well-structured argument that somehow manages to say absolutely nothing. And you think: "Ugh. AI-generated garbage."

And you know what? Sometimes you're right. A lot of AI content is indeed garbage. But here's the thing that should really piss you off: a lot of human-generated content is garbage too.

The Authenticity Fetish

We've developed this weird fetish for "authenticity" in content creation. It's like we've collectively decided that the how matters more than the what. That a mediocre idea typed by calloused human fingers is somehow more valuable than a brilliant insight organized by silicon and algorithms.

This is the same species, by the way, that spends $8 on coffee because the barista has a man-bun and "hand-crafts" each cup. Meanwhile, the 7-Eleven coffee is made by a machine and costs $1.50. Sometimes it tastes better! But we don't want to admit that because we've invested so much emotional energy into believing that human involvement automatically equals quality.

It doesn't.

What We're Really Afraid Of

You know what's really happening here? We're afraid that if AI can write as well as we can, then maybe‚Äîjust maybe‚Äîwe weren't that special to begin with. And that's terrifying. Because if a blog post about productivity hacks or sourdough starter or whatever doesn't need the human touch, then what does that say about the person who spent three hours writing one last Tuesday?

It says that maybe your three hours weren't adding the value you thought they were.

The craftsman who spent decades perfecting wooden wheels probably felt the same way about the assembly line. The switchboard operator probably had thoughts about automated exchanges. And they were all technically correct‚Äîthe old way DID have a human touch. It was just that nobody actually needed that particular human touch once they realized the alternative worked fine.

The Only Question That Matters

Here's my radical proposition: Judge content by whether it's true and useful, not by who‚Äîor what‚Äîcreated it.

Did this blog post teach you something you didn't know? Did it change your perspective? Did it make you laugh, or think, or feel less alone? Then who gives a damn if it was written by a person, an AI, or a very motivated parrot with a keyboard?

I mean, we already accept this principle everywhere else. You don't refuse penicillin because it was made in a factory instead of hand-harvested by monks. You don't reject the theory of relativity because Einstein didn't write it in iambic pentameter while sitting under a tree. We judge medicine by whether it works. We judge science by whether it's true.

But somehow, when it comes to content, we've decided that the process matters more than the product.

The Real Problem Isn't AI

Look, here's what actually makes content bad:
- It's wrong
- It's useless
- It's boring
- It says nothing new
- It wastes your time

Notice what's not on that list? How it was made.

The real problem with most AI-generated content isn't that it's AI-generated. It's that it's optimized for search engines instead of humans. It's that it's created at scale by people who don't care about quality. It's that it's designed to fill space rather than minds.

But you know what? Humans have been doing that for centuries. We just called it "content marketing" or "journalism" or "my cousin's Facebook posts."

The Hypocrisy Is Delicious

And here's the kicker‚Äîthe same people who clutch their pearls about AI-generated blog posts are perfectly fine with:
- Spell-checkers fixing their grammar
- Grammarly restructuring their sentences
- Autocorrect choosing their words
- Templates organizing their thoughts
- Google suggesting their searches
- Editors rewriting their drafts

At what point, exactly, does the "human creation" become "AI assistance" become "AI generation"? Is there a percentage we're tracking here? If AI writes 49% of your blog post, is it still authentic? What about 51%?

We're drawing these arbitrary lines in the sand and pretending they mean something. Meanwhile, the tide of technology is coming in, and it does not care about our lines.

The Future Is Already Here

Here's the uncomfortable truth: in ten years, the question "was this made by AI?" will sound as quaint as asking "was this typed on a typewriter or a computer?"

Nobody cares if you used a hammer or a nail gun to build your deck. They care if the deck holds their weight.

Nobody cares if you used film or digital to take your photograph. They care if it captures something worth seeing.

And soon, nobody will care if you used your brain or an AI to write your blog post. They'll care if it was worth reading.

So What Now?

If you're a content creator freaking out about AI, I have good news and bad news.

Bad news: If your value proposition is "I can arrange words into sentences," you're cooked. That's over. AI can do that now, and it'll only get better.

Good news: If your value proposition is "I have unique experiences, perspectives, and insights that others don't," you're fine. AI can't replicate what it's like to be you. Yet.

The solution isn't to demand that people label their AI-generated content like it's a GMO tomato. The solution is to create content that's so true, so useful, so insightful that nobody cares how it got made.

Because at the end of the day, knowledge is knowledge. Truth is truth. And if an AI can deliver it better than you can, then maybe‚Äîjust maybe‚Äîthe problem isn't the AI.

The problem is that you weren't delivering much value to begin with.

And if you're wondering whether THIS was written by AI... well, does it matter? Did you learn something? Did it make you think? Then shut up and share it.
