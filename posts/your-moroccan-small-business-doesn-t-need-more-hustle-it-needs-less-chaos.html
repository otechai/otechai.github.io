The Lazy Hacker's Take on Actually Running a Business

I'm lazy. Productively lazy. The kind of lazy where I'll spend three hours automating something that takes ten minutes daily, because I've done the math and I'm never doing that boring thing manually again.

Most business software is built by people who've never actually run a business. It shows. You end up with seventeen different apps that don't talk to each other, subscriptions that add up to more than your rent, and still somehow you're manually copying data between systems like it's 2003.

Odoo is different because it's built on a premise I deeply respect: everything in your business touches everything else. Your sales affect your inventory. Your inventory affects your purchasing. Your purchasing affects your accounting. Your accounting affects your... okay, you get it.

Instead of duct-taping together QuickBooks + some inventory app + a separate CRM + prayer, you get one system where information flows naturally. Create an invoice? This matters more in Morocco than almost anywhere else, because we're operating in a market that demands flexibility. See, most small business owners in Morocco don't need 80% of what Odoo offers. The paid version (Odoo Enterprise) adds some nice features and official support, but honestly? Start with community. If you outgrow it, that's a good problem to have.

The Real Obstacle (It's Not What You Think)

The actual barrier to small businesses in Morocco using something like Odoo isn't cost, or complexity, or technical requirements. It's mindset.

We're so used to hustling, to grinding, to manually doing everything ourselves because "that's how it's done," that we don't stop to ask: should it be done this way?

There's this weird badge of honor around working harder instead of smarter. Like if you're not drowning in administrative tasks, you're not really working. That's nonsense. That's the kind of thinking that keeps your business small.

The goal isn't to work less (though that's nice). The goal is to work on things that actually matter. Strategic thinking. Customer relationships. Product development. Not manually entering the same data three times because your systems don't talk to each other.

Automation isn't lazy. Manual repetitive work when automation exists is lazy. It's intellectual laziness‚Äîchoosing the familiar pain over the temporary discomfort of learning something better.
