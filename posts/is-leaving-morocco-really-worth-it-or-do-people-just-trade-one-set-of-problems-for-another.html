The Visa Fallacy: Are We Escaping or Just Trading Cages?

January 05, 2026

If you sit in any café in Casablanca, Rabat, or Oujda for long enough, the conversation inevitably drifts toward the "Exit." It is the Moroccan national sport, ranking just above football and complaining about the weather. The dream is vivid: a one-way ticket to Canada, France, or Germany, where the streets are paved with dignity, the hospitals actually cure you, and the police say "please."

I understand this visceral pull. I feel it too. When you watch a loved one wait six months for an MRI scan, or when a police officer treats you like a criminal for simply walking down the street, the rage builds up in your chest like steam in a pressure cooker. Leaving feels less like a choice and more like an evacuation.

But there is a rather uncomfortable question we rarely ask in those café debates, mostly because it ruins the fantasy: Is the grass actually greener, or is it just a different shade of brown?

Are we solving our problems, or are we simply engaging in a high-stakes currency exchange—trading our chaos for loneliness, and our frustration for invisibility?

The Mathematics of Illusion

Let's start with the money, because that's usually the headline act. The math seems simple enough to a 24-year-old software engineer. In Casablanca, you might earn 15,000 DH ($1,500). In Paris or Berlin, you are offered €3,500. It looks like a no-brainer. It looks like wealth.

But this is where we confuse income with purchasing power. We forget the "Western Tax"—and I don't just mean the literal tax (though seeing 40% of your paycheck vanish before it hits your account is a spiritual experience in its own right).

I mean the cost of existence. In Morocco, if your sink breaks, you call a guy who fixes it for 100 DH and a glass of tea. In London, that plumber charges £150 just to ring the doorbell. In Morocco, childcare is often a mix of family, neighbors, and affordable help. In the West, childcare is a second mortgage.

Many who leave find themselves on a relentless hamster wheel. They have the iPhone 17 and the leased Audi, but they are living paycheck to paycheck, terrified of a dental emergency. They have traded "poverty" (which, let's be honest, for the middle class in Morocco is relative) for a shiny, high-definition version of working-poor anxiety.

The Tax on the Soul: Al-Ghorba

Then there is the silence. This is the part nobody puts on Instagram.

Moroccan society is loud, chaotic, and invasive. Your neighbors know what you cooked for lunch. Your aunt has an opinion on your haircut. It can be suffocating. But—and this is a massive "but"—it is a safety net made of people.

When you move to a hyper-individualistic society, you gain privacy, yes. But you lose the tribe. You trade the intrusive aunt for the neighbor who has lived next door for five years and still hasn't learned your name. You trade the chaotic noise of the medina for a silence so loud it rings in your ears on Sunday afternoons.

We often underestimate how much of our mental health is outsourced to our community. In the West, you have to generate your own happiness, entirely from scratch, every single day. That is exhausting work.

I have friends in prestigious positions in Europe who admit, after three drinks, that they are profoundly lonely. They have "made it," but they have no one to celebrate with. They are winning a game that nobody is watching.

The Myth of Dignity

The strongest argument for leaving is usually Karama—dignity. We leave to escape Hogra (injustice/humiliation). We want to live in a state of law.

And to be fair, there is a profound relief in living where the bus comes on time and the bureaucracy works (mostly). It heals something inside you to interact with a state that doesn't view you as an adversary.

However, we must not be naïve. You do not magically become a "citizen of the world" when you land at JFK or Heathrow. You become an immigrant. You trade the classism of Morocco for the subtle, systemic racism of the West.

In Morocco, you might be judged for your last name or your shoe size. In Europe, you are judged for your skin tone, your accent, and your religion. You go from being a frustrated citizen to being a "problem." You have to work twice as hard to be considered half as competent. Is that dignity? Or is it just a different flavor of humiliation—one that is colder, more polite, but equally corrosive?

The Golden Handcuffs

The tragedy is that once you realize this, it is often too late to come back easily. This is the trap of the "Golden Handcuffs."

You get used to the efficiency. You get used to the clean parks and the fast internet. You have kids who speak German or Dutch better than Darija. You look at Morocco and you miss the warmth, but you dread the chaos. You become stuck in the middle of the Atlantic Ocean—too Moroccan for Europe, too European for Morocco.

You become a permanent tourist in your own life.

So, Is It Worth It?

I am not writing this to tell you to stay. Staying in an environment that crushes your potential is not noble; it is masochistic. If you are drowning, you must swim to shore, wherever that shore is.

But we must stop treating emigration as a solution. It is not a solution; it is a trade-off. It is a set of compromises.

Leaving is worth it if:

  • You are running toward something specific (a niche career, a specific education, a love), not just running away from frustration.
  • You are willing to accept that you will always be a guest in someone else's house.
  • You understand that money is not the same as wealth.

Leaving is a trap if:

  • You think geography will fix your internal unhappiness. (Spoiler: You take your depression with you in your carry-on luggage).
  • You expect the West to be a meritocracy without prejudice.
  • You undervalue the immense, invisible wealth of having a family and a culture that understands you without explanation.

The Third Way

Perhaps the most subversive act in 2026 is not to leave, and not to submit, but to build your own pocket of sanity right here. To create excellence in a place that accepts mediocrity. To build your own "West" within the chaos of the "East."

It is bloody hard work. It requires a stubbornness that borders on insanity. But there is a specific kind of glory in succeeding on your own soil, in speaking your own language, in being near your mother when she gets old.

The grass isn't greener on the other side. The grass is greener where you water it. And sometimes, the water bill in Europe is just too damn high.
