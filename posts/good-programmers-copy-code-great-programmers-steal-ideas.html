Good programmers copy code, great programmers steal ideas

There is a rather cheeky aphorism often attributed to Pablo Picasso, though T.S. Eliot probably said it better, and Steve Jobs definitely said it loudest: "Good artists copy; great artists steal."

In the world of software development, where we sit in ergonomic chairs typing incantations that make sand think, this quote isn't just a witty observation. It is the fundamental law of physics that governs our profession.

But let’s be honest—it sounds terrible, doesn't it? It sounds like an endorsement of plagiarism, a nod to intellectual laziness, or a justification for corporate espionage. If you say this to a layman, they clutch their pearls. If you say it to a junior developer, they look confused.

However, if you whisper it to a Senior Engineer who has been debugging a race condition for three days on a caffeine-only diet, they will simply nod. They know.

The distinction between "copying" and "stealing" in code is not a matter of legality; it is a matter of metabolism. It is the difference between a parrot repeating a phrase it heard on the television and a poet using a common word to break your heart.

Let’s dismantle this, shall we? Because understanding this distinction might just save your career—and your sanity.

The Cargo Cult of the Copy-Paste

We have all been the "Bad Copier." It is a rite of passage.

You are twenty-two years old. The deadline was yesterday. The error message on your screen is red, angry, and cryptic—something about a "NullReferenceException" or a "Segfault" that feels like a personal insult from the compiler. You panic. You go to the Oracle, also known as Stack Overflow.

You find a thread from 2014. A user named CodeNinja99 has posted a solution. You don't read it. You don't understand it. You highlight, Ctrl+C, Ctrl+V. You run the code. It works. The red text turns green. You push the commit and go to the pub.

This is copying. And it is dangerous.

It is dangerous not because it is "cheating," but because it is fragile. You have imported a solution without importing the understanding. You have engaged in what the physicist Richard Feynman called "Cargo Cult Science"—building the shape of an airfield and hoping the planes land, without understanding the mechanics of flight.

When you copy, you inherit the solution, yes. But you also inherit the hidden constraints, the edge cases, and the technical debt of the original author. You are Frankenstein, stitching together limbs you found in the graveyard, hoping the lightning strikes correctly. And when that code breaks six months later (and it will), you will stare at it with the same blank horror as the day you pasted it, because you never really owned it.

Copying is superficial. It is the act of a tourist taking a photo of the Eiffel Tower. You were there, you have the proof, but you don't know the city.

The Art of the Grand Heist

Now, let’s look at the "Great Steal."

To steal an idea in programming is to ingest it. It is to look at a piece of code, a design pattern, or an architecture, and take it apart until you see the clockwork gears turning underneath.

When a great programmer "steals," they are not looking for a shortcut. They are looking for a paradigm.

Consider the most famous heist in tech history: The Graphical User Interface (GUI). In 1979, Steve Jobs and his team visited Xerox PARC. They saw the Alto, a computer with windows, a mouse, and icons. Xerox management thought it was a neat toy for secretaries. Jobs saw the future of humanity.

He didn't just copy the pixels. If he had, the Macintosh would have been a slow, expensive clone of the Xerox Alto. Instead, he stole the concept. He absorbed the philosophy of "direct manipulation," went back to Apple, and engineered it to be faster, cheaper, and intuitive. Then, Bill Gates saw what Apple did, and he stole it for Windows.

Was it fair? Perhaps not. Was it legal? The lawyers are still arguing. But was it progress? Absolutely.

Great stealing requires a level of humility that is rare in our ego-driven industry. It requires you to admit, "I cannot write a better sorting algorithm than the one in the standard library." It requires you to look at an open-source library that thousands of people have worked on and say, "I will use this foundation rather than trying to be a hero and build my own from scratch."

There is a profound dignity in this. It is an acknowledgement that we are standing on the shoulders of giants. When you use an open-source tool like React or Linux, you are essentially stealing the collective intelligence of thousands of brilliant minds. You are leveraging their sleepless nights so you can have a restful one.

A thief, in this context, is a craftsman who respects their tools too much to reinvent them poorly.

The AI Elephant in the Room

We cannot have this conversation in 2025 without addressing the synthetic elephant in the room: Artificial Intelligence.

Tools like ChatGPT and Copilot have industrialized the act of copying. They are the ultimate "Bad Copiers." They can generate boilerplate code faster than you can blink. And this is where the danger lies.

If you use AI to generate code you do not understand, you are not a programmer; you are a content manager for a machine. You are flooding the world with "slop"—code that looks correct on the surface but has no soul, no intentionality, and often, subtle, hallucinations that will wake you up at 3 AM on a Saturday.

However, the "Great Thief" uses AI differently. They use it as a sparring partner. They ask it for ideas, they look at the output, they critique it, they strip it for parts, and they implement the core logic with their own understanding. They treat the AI not as an oracle, but as a junior developer who is very fast but slightly drunk.

The thief maintains agency. The copier surrenders it.

The Moral Economy of Code

There is a deeper, almost spiritual lesson here about how we relate to truth and property.

In the West, we are obsessed with the "Lone Genius" myth. We want to believe that Elon Musk built the rocket with his own hands, or that Mark Zuckerberg wrote every line of Facebook. We love the idea of the original creator.

But code, like jazz, is a communal art form. There is no such thing as "original" code. Every line you write rests on an operating system you didn't build, compiled by a language you didn't invent, running on hardware you don't understand, powered by physics you can't explain.

To try and claim total originality is not just arrogant; it is a denial of reality.

The great programmer understands that their job is not to be the smartest person in the room. Their job is to solve the problem. And if the solution already exists, the most ethical, efficient, and "truthful" thing to do is to adapt that solution.

But here is the covenant: If you steal, you must improve. Or at the very least, you must understand.

If you take a library, read the documentation. If you copy a function, refactor it to fit your style guide. If you use an architecture, understand why it was built that way.

When you steal with understanding, you honor the original creator. You are saying, "Your idea was so robust, so elegant, that I am making it a part of my own toolkit." You are keeping the idea alive.

When you copy without thinking, you are insulting the creator. You are treating their work as a magic spell, reducing their intellect to a utility.

Conclusion: Be a Gentleman Thief

So, how do we apply this? How do we walk the line between plagiarism and innovation?

  1. Don’t Reinvent the Wheel (Unless You’re Studying Wheels): If you need to encrypt a password, steal the standard library implementation. Do not write your own crypto. That is not creativity; that is security suicide.
  2. Read the Source Code: Don’t just read the tutorial. Go into the GitHub repository. See how the sausage is made. The best way to steal a master’s technique is to watch their hands.
  3. Credit Your Sources: Even thieves can have manners. If you use a snippet from a blog post, put a comment in the code with the link. It helps the next poor soul who has to maintain your code.
  4. Own the Failure: If the code you "stole" breaks, it is your fault. You cannot blame the original author. Once you paste it, you bought it.

In the end, programming is an act of synthesis. We are weaving together threads of logic that have been spun by Ada Lovelace, Grace Hopper, Linus Torvalds, and that anonymous user on Stack Overflow who solved your problem seven years ago.

Don't be a bad copier, mindlessly echoing the noise. Be a great thief. Take the best ideas humanity has to offer, understand them, polish them, and use them to build something that actually works.

The world doesn't need more complex code. It needs more working solutions. It needs more light. And if you have to steal a little fire from the gods to keep the lights on, well... Prometheus would be proud.
